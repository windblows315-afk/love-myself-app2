Ask for what you need. Give what you can. Stay yourself.
The three styles
Passive
You stay silent about what you need to avoid conflict or to keep the peace. Your feelings get buried. Others can't know what you want if you never say it.
Sounds like: "It's fine, don't worry about it." · "No no you pick." · "I don't mind." (when you do)
Aggressive
You push your needs in a way that disregards the other person. It may get results short-term but damages trust and connection.
Sounds like: "You always do this." · "Why can't you just" · demanding, blaming, raising your voice.
Assertive ✦
You express your needs clearly and kindly. You respect yourself and the other person. You can say no without guilt, and yes without resentment.
Sounds like: "I feel ___ when ___, and I need ___." · "I can't do that, but I can ___." · "That doesn't work for me."
Why it's worth learning
You stop abandoning yourself to manage other people's feelings.
Your relationships get more real people know where you actually stand.
Resentment has less room to build up.
You model healthy communication for everyone around you.
How to ask for what you need
The formula
"When [situation], I feel [emotion], because I need [need]. Would you be willing to [request]?"
Real examples
"When plans change last minute, I feel anxious because I need some predictability. Could you let me know at least a day ahead when you can?"
"I've been feeling really overwhelmed. I need some alone time tonight not because of you, just to recharge."
"That comment hurt a bit. I know you didn't mean it that way, but I'd love it if we could talk about it."
Things to remember
You're allowed to have needs. Needs aren't demands.
Say "I feel" not "you make me feel" it keeps it yours.
Be specific about what you're asking for. Vague asks get vague responses.
Their reaction is theirs. You're responsible for how you ask, not how they respond.

How to give to others without losing yourself
The key idea
Giving from fullness is love. Giving from fear of what happens if you don't is people-pleasing.
Assertive giving looks like
Saying yes because you want to, not to avoid guilt.
Saying no with warmth "I can't this time, but I care about you."
Offering what you genuinely have energy, time, attention without overextending.
Checking in with yourself first: Do I actually have capacity for this right now?
Not over-explaining or apologizing for your limits.
Ways to say no kindly
"I really wish I could I just don't have the bandwidth right now."
"That's not something I can commit to, but I appreciate you asking."
"I can't do [that], but I can do [this] would that help?"
"No, but I love you." (sometimes that's enough)
Reflect what do I tend to over-give in?

You don't have to earn the right to believe in yourself. But here's how to build it anyway.
The truth about self-belief
Self-belief is not about being sure you'll succeed. It's about trusting that you can handle whatever happens.
You do not need to
Achieve a certain number of things before you're allowed to believe in yourself.
Prove your worth to anyone, including yourself.
Be without doubt in order to move forward.
Have it all figured out first.
What actually builds self-belief
Keeping promises to yourself
Every time you do what you said you'd do, even something small, your inner self registers it. You become someone you can count on.
Trying things before you feel ready
Confidence comes after action, not before. You don't wait until you believe in yourself to try. You try, and that's how you start to believe.
Noticing what you've already done
Your brain is wired to forget your wins and remember your failures. You have to actively remind yourself of your own story.
Talking to yourself like someone you love
The voice in your head shapes what you believe about yourself more than any achievement ever could.
What does believing in yourself mean to you?
You don't need a list of achievements to believe in yourself. But collecting evidence of who you are helps your brain catch up to the truth.
Evidence isn't only big things
You showed up when you didn't feel like it.
You got through something that felt impossible.
You chose yourself in a moment when you used to erase yourself.
You kept going even when no one was watching.
You tried something new even though it scared you.
Add a piece of evidence
Big or small. What's something you did that proves you are more capable than you think?
When things don't go the way you hoped
Failure is not proof that you can't. It's proof that you tried. And trying is exactly what self-belief looks like in practice.
What to do right after a failure
1. Feel it without making it mean everything
Disappointment is real. Let yourself feel it. But there's a difference between "that didn't work" and "I am a failure." One is a fact. The other is a story.
2. Separate what happened from who you are
You are not your results. A bad outcome doesn't rewrite your character, your effort, or your worth. Those stay.
3. Ask what it taught you, not what it proved
Instead of "what does this say about me?" ask "what does this show me about what I can do differently?" One shrinks you. The other grows you.
4. Remind yourself of your evidence
Go back to your Evidence tab. Read what you wrote. One failure doesn't erase all of that. Your track record is still yours.
5. Choose to keep going anyway
This is the moment self-belief is actually built. Not in the wins, but in the decision to try again after losing. That decision is yours alone.
Things to say to yourself after a failure
"I tried. That already took courage."
"This is one moment, not my whole story."
"I am still here. That counts for something."
"What can I learn from this that makes me better?"
"I don't have to feel confident right now. I just have to keep going."
Process a failure
Write out what happened, how you feel, what it taught you, and what you're choosing to do next.

What is Calisthenics?
Calisthenics means using your own bodyweight as resistance no gym, no equipment. Push-ups, squats, planks that's calisthenics. It builds real strength, flexibility, and body control at your own pace.
What does "3 sets × 10" mean?
Sets = how many times you repeat a group of reps. Reps = how many times you do one movement. So 3 sets × 10 reps means: do the exercise 10 times, rest, do it again, rest, do it one more time. That's it 🤍
